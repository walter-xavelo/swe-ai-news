## Overview

A February 28 **ZDNET** article highlighted a more grounded trend in AI-assisted software engineering: teams are no longer just asking whether coding copilots work, but **how to use them without degrading code quality**.

The piece centered on practical AI coding techniques for shipping real products quickly while keeping reliability in view. That framing matters because it mirrors what many engineering teams are learning firsthand in 2026: the productivity gains are real, but they depend heavily on workflow discipline.

Typical themes in this category of coverage include:

- using AI to accelerate scaffolding and boilerplate rather than blindly owning core logic,
- tightening prompt context around specs and constraints,
- validating model output with tests and human review,
- and keeping reliability, maintainability, and deploy safety as first-class concerns.

## Why This Matters

The significance here is less about one tool launch and more about a visible **maturity shift in developer practice**. AI coding is no longer interesting only because it is fast; it is interesting because teams are figuring out which patterns actually hold up in production.

That makes this a relevant Feb. 28 signal for SWE/AI readers: the conversation is moving from demo-speed generation to **repeatable engineering workflows** that blend copilots, testing, review, and deployment guardrails.
